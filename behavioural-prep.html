Technical skill gets you in the room. Behavioural answers get you the offer. Companies like Amazon weight these as heavily as coding. The secret is preparing real stories in advance using the STAR method.

01The STAR method — your framework

Every behavioural answer should follow this structure. It keeps you focused and ensures you actually answer the question instead of rambling.

S
Situation
Set the scene briefly. What was the context? Keep it to 1-2 sentences.
T
Task
What was your responsibility or the challenge you faced?
A
Action
What did YOU specifically do? Use "I" not "we". This is the longest part.
R
Result
What was the outcome? Quantify it if possible. What did you learn?
The number one mistake
Candidates say "we" too much. The interviewer wants to know what YOU did. Even in a team effort, be clear about your specific contribution. Say "I designed the transaction flow" not "we built the system".
02Prepare these stories in advance

Most behavioural questions map to a handful of story types. Prepare 5-6 solid stories from your Mainframe and university experience, and you can adapt them to almost any question.

The essential questions
Must prepTell me about yourself▶
How to answer

This is your opening pitch — 60-90 seconds. Structure: present → past → future.

Present: "I'm a software engineer at Mainframe, where I build full-stack systems for a live betting platform using Angular and .NET Core."

Past: "I have a Computer Science degree from Peradeniya where I focused on machine learning — my thesis was on network intrusion detection. I started at Mainframe as an intern and was converted to full-time based on my delivery."

Future: "Now I'm looking to take on bigger challenges at scale, which is what drew me to this role."

Don't
Don't recite your entire CV chronologically. Don't talk about personal life. Keep it professional, tight, and end with why you're excited about THIS role.
Must prepTell me about a challenging technical problem you solved▶
Your story bank — betting transaction integrity

S: "On the betting platform, we needed bet placement to be completely reliable — placing a bet and deducting the user's balance had to happen atomically."

T: "My task was to build this flow ensuring no scenario could leave a user with a placed bet but no balance deducted, or vice versa, even under concurrent requests."

A: "I implemented the flow using database transactions so both operations committed together or rolled back together. I also handled the concurrency case to prevent double-spending of balance."

R: "The result was a reliable transaction flow with no consistency issues in production. It taught me how critical correctness is when real money is involved."

Must prepTell me about a time you had a conflict with a colleague▶
How to approach it

Interviewers want to see maturity, not that you never disagree. Show you can handle disagreement professionally and reach a good outcome.

Framework: describe a genuine technical disagreement (e.g. an approach to a feature), explain how you listened to their view, presented your reasoning with data or examples, and reached a resolution together — ideally one that improved on both original ideas.

Key: end by showing you prioritised the outcome and the relationship over being "right".

Avoid
Never badmouth the colleague or make them the villain. Never pick a conflict where you were clearly wrong or behaved badly. Show emotional maturity.
Must prepTell me about a time you failed or made a mistake▶
How to approach it

They want to see accountability and growth — not perfection. The structure that works: own the mistake honestly, explain the impact, describe what you did to fix it, and most importantly what you changed afterward so it never happened again.

Good example type: a bug that reached production, a missed edge case, an underestimated timeline. Something real but not catastrophic.

The golden rule
Always end a failure story with the lesson and how you apply it now. The failure is the setup; the growth is the point. That's what they're actually evaluating.
Must prepTell me about a time you took initiative / went beyond your role▶
Your story bank options
  • Building the interactive learning resources and documentation at Mainframe beyond your assigned tasks
  • The NX monorepo migration — modernising the frontend architecture proactively
  • Taking on the mobile backend API work in addition to web development

Pick one, frame it in STAR, and emphasise that you saw a need and acted on it without being asked.

Must prepWhy do you want to work here / why are you leaving?▶
How to answer

Why here: Be specific to the company. Research their products, engineering culture, or tech stack. "I'm drawn to the scale of problems you work on and your engineering culture around X."

Why leaving: Always stay positive about your current role. Frame it as moving toward growth, not running away. "I've learned a huge amount at Mainframe building production systems, and now I want to work on larger-scale challenges with a bigger engineering team."

Never
Never badmouth your current employer, salary complaints, or "I'm bored". These are all red flags. Always frame the move as toward something, not away from something.
03Amazon Leadership Principles (if targeting Amazon)

Amazon interviews are built entirely around 16 Leadership Principles. Every behavioural question maps to one. The most commonly tested ones:

AmazonThe key principles to prepare stories for▶
  • Customer Obsession — a time you prioritised the user's needs
  • Ownership — a time you took responsibility beyond your role
  • Invent and Simplify — a time you simplified something complex
  • Dive Deep — a time you dug into details to find a root cause
  • Bias for Action — a time you moved fast under uncertainty
  • Deliver Results — a time you delivered despite obstacles
  • Earn Trust — a time you built trust with a team or handled a disagreement

Prepare one strong STAR story for each. Many of your Mainframe experiences can map to several of these.

04Questions to ask THEM

Always have questions ready. "No questions" signals disinterest. Good ones:

  • "What does success look like in this role in the first 6 months?"
  • "What are the biggest technical challenges the team is facing right now?"
  • "How does the team approach code review and technical decisions?"
  • "What does the deployment and on-call process look like?"
  • "What growth opportunities exist for engineers here?"
