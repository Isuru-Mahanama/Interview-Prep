Salary Negotiation

Negotiating
your offer

The single highest-value hour of your job search. Most engineers accept the first number out of fear. A calm, prepared negotiation can add significantly to your compensation — and companies expect it. This guide gives you the mindset and the exact words.

01The mindset — before anything else

Negotiating is expected. Recruiters make a first offer assuming you'll negotiate. Accepting immediately often means leaving money on the table that was already budgeted for you.

It won't cost you the offer. A polite, reasonable negotiation almost never makes a company rescind. They already decided they want you — that's why they made an offer.

The company has more information than you. They know the salary band; you often don't. Your job is to close that information gap before committing to any number.

The one rule that matters most
Never give a specific number first if you can avoid it. Whoever names a number first anchors the negotiation — and it's usually not in your favour. Deflect the "what's your expected salary" question until you have an offer.
02Handling "What's your expected salary?"

This question usually comes early — often from a recruiter in the first call. Your goal is to deflect gracefully without giving a number.

Deflect — best option
"I'd love to learn more about the role and responsibilities first before discussing numbers. I'm confident we can find a package that works for both of us if I'm the right fit. Could you share the budgeted range for this position?"
If they push for a number
"I'm focused on finding the right opportunity, and compensation is one part of that. I'd rather understand the full picture first — but if it helps, I'm looking for a competitive package for a senior full-stack engineer with my experience. What range did you have in mind?"
If they absolutely insist
Give a researched range, not a single number, and anchor slightly high: "Based on my research for this kind of role, I'd expect somewhere in the range of X to Y — but I'm flexible depending on the overall package."
Do your homework first
Research salary ranges before any call using Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, and LinkedIn Salary for the specific company, role, and location. Know the market rate so you can recognise a low offer and justify a counter.
03When you get the offer
Step 1Never accept on the spot — even a great offer▶

Always thank them warmly and ask for time to consider. This is completely normal and expected. Accepting instantly signals you'd have taken less.

Say this
"Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this opportunity. This is an important decision, so I'd appreciate a few days to review the full offer. Could I get back to you by [date]?"
Step 2Make your counter — the actual ask▶

Counter with confidence and a reason. Anchor above your target so there's room to settle. Always be specific and professional.

The counter script
"Thank you again for the offer — I'm very excited to join. Based on my experience building production full-stack systems, my research on market rates, and the value I'll bring, I was hoping we could get the base salary to [your target, anchored a bit high]. Is there flexibility there?"
Keep it short
After you make the ask, stop talking. Silence is powerful. Don't over-justify or apologise. Let them respond.
Step 3Negotiate the whole package, not just base salary▶

If they can't move on base salary, other things are often negotiable:

  • Signing bonus — often easier to grant than raising base
  • Equity / stock — significant at bigger companies
  • Annual bonus target
  • Remote/flexible working arrangements
  • Extra vacation days
  • Learning/conference budget
  • Earlier salary review — e.g. a review at 6 months instead of 12
LeverageUsing a competing offer (if you have one)▶

A competing offer is the strongest leverage. Use it honestly and without threats.

Say this
"I want to be transparent — I have another offer on the table, but your company is genuinely my first choice. If we could close the gap on compensation, I'd be ready to sign immediately."
Never bluff
Don't invent a competing offer. If they call your bluff (e.g. "can you send the offer letter?"), you lose all credibility. Only use real leverage.
04Special note for your situation

If you're targeting international or remote roles from Sri Lanka: compensation varies enormously by company location and whether they pay local or global rates. Research whether the company pays location-adjusted or global salaries — this hugely affects what's reasonable to ask.

Your leverage points: 2.5+ years of production experience, full-stack range (Angular + .NET + SQL), a strong CS degree with an ML thesis, and rare bridge skills (ML + software engineering). These justify asking for the upper end of a range, not the middle.

Don't undervalue yourself. Engineers from smaller markets often anchor too low out of habit. If you're applying to global companies, benchmark against global rates for the role, not local ones.
